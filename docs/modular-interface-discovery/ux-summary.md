# FUN-TASTIC Modular Interface - UX Summary

Checked: 2026-07-18

## UX Goal

The target experience is not maximum retention. It is a short, understandable sequence that gives the user confidence and a small moment of delight:

> approach -> self-align -> complete one obvious motion -> receive tactile/audible confirmation -> see that the module is correctly seated

The interface must not resemble LEGO studs, brick proportions, clutch cylinders or recognizable brick geometry. The desired reference is the joy of assembly, not LEGO's appearance or interface.

## What Creates a Satisfying Click

A click is normally produced when a compliant element stores elastic energy while moving over a ramp or obstruction, then releases that energy suddenly into a recess or stop. The abrupt motion and contact create vibration, sound and a clear change in hand force.

The satisfying part is not sound alone. It is the combination of:

- Increasing resistance before engagement
- A short and predictable peak force
- Sudden reduction in force
- A hard positional stop
- Audible or tactile confirmation
- Little rattle after engagement

Magnetic-mechanical systems add self-alignment before the mechanical click. FIDLOCK explicitly describes magnets guiding the parts, followed by an audible mechanical lock and one-hand release. This is a strong UX benchmark, but not a form to copy.

## Common Traits of High-Satisfaction Interfaces

1. **One dominant motion:** slide, rotate, press or tilt, rather than several hidden steps.
2. **Self-alignment:** the interface corrects small approach errors before locking.
3. **Progressive resistance:** force rises predictably rather than jamming immediately.
4. **End-state confirmation:** sound, force drop, visible alignment or all three.
5. **Reversible logic:** detachment is related to attachment and easy to remember.
6. **Low ambiguity:** the module cannot appear installed while only partly engaged.
7. **Stable rest state:** the module does not rattle, rotate or lift during normal use.
8. **Forgiving handling:** users do not need to inspect a hidden connector closely.

## Beginner Comprehension

The most intuitive families are:

- Gravity drop-in: place and release
- Hook-and-slide: hang and move downward
- Guided slide: align with a visible path and move to an end stop
- Short-turn bayonet: insert, then follow an obvious short rotation
- Magnetic-mechanical: bring close and allow self-guidance

Pure press fits and hidden cantilever snaps are less predictable because users cannot tell how much force is safe. Multi-turn threads are familiar but slow. Cam locks and ball detents can feel mechanical rather than cute and simple.

## Cute Brand Compatibility

Cute and playful identity should come from the interaction language around the interface, not from copying a toy brick.

Suitable brand cues:

- Rounded module silhouettes independent of the connector
- Color contrast that identifies the attach/release area
- Simple directional icons
- A visible completion mark when fully seated
- Short motion with a restrained click
- Mix-and-match color sets
- Small labels, names or character-free decorative caps

Unsuitable cues:

- Stud arrays
- Brick-like rectangular proportions
- Cylindrical clutch geometry
- A color/shape combination likely to imply LEGO compatibility
- Excessively loud or high-force clicks that feel like a tool rather than a desk object

## One-Hand Use

One-hand attachment is easier than one-hand removal. The removal action should not require holding the Base with the other hand. This favors interfaces where the Base transfers load to the desk or where release force acts along the guided path rather than pulling the Base away.

The one-hand UX must be verified with:

- Dominant and non-dominant hand
- Short and long fingernails
- Low vision and first-time use
- Base lightly loaded and empty
- Module at the edge and center of the Base
- At least 100 repeated user cycles during early testing, followed by a longer engineering cycle test

No force target is set in this market phase; it requires physical prototypes and user testing.

## UX Recommendation

Prioritize a **guided slide with an end-state detent**. Keep **hook-and-slide with a terminal catch** as the low-risk fallback. Test a **short-turn bayonet with detent** as the most playful alternative.

These are interface families, not proprietary geometries.
