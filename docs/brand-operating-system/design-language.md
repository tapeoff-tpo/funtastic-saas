# FUN-TASTIC Design Language

Checked: 2026-07-18

## Intent

The design language should make modules feel friendly, understandable, composable, and unmistakably part of one living platform. This document defines philosophy and behavior, not product geometry or CAD.

## Experience Attributes

- Friendly, not childish
- Compact, not miniature for its own sake
- Soft, not vague
- Playful, not toy-like
- Expressive, not visually noisy
- Simple, not feature-poor
- Familiar, not generic
- Premium through care, not decorative excess

## Interaction Language

### Guided

The user should perceive where the module goes and how the motion starts.

### Confirmed

Attachment should end with a clear tactile, audible, or visible state where appropriate.

### Reversible

Removal should feel intentional and understandable without threatening breakage.

### Fluent

The same interaction principle should transfer across Zones and module functions.

### Forgiving

Alignment and force should tolerate normal user and FDM variation after validation.

Joy should come from growing fluency and composition, not unnecessary mechanical steps.

## Form Behavior

- Use compact visual mass and clear functional hierarchy.
- Favor transitions that look approachable and are easy to clean within the validated Zone.
- Let the connection state remain legible without exposing technical clutter.
- Use one dominant gesture per module rather than many decorative features.
- Preserve family resemblance through proportion behavior and junction treatment, not identical silhouettes.
- Avoid LEGO-like studs, brick ratios, minifigure cues, or construction-toy mimicry.
- Avoid fake material textures and cosmetic complexity that increase print or cleaning burden without value.

No radius, dimension, curvature, or tolerance is specified in this phase.

## CMF Philosophy

### Base

- Visually stable
- Primarily neutral
- Designed to support mixed module colors
- Environment and material class must remain identifiable

### Module

- Carries functional and personal expression
- Uses controlled color families rather than unlimited one-off shades
- May use contrast to clarify grip, orientation, category, or status

### Finish

- Consistency across batches matters more than chasing a generic “premium” gloss level
- Low-gloss intent can support calm interiors, subject to print, wear, cleaning, and photography validation
- Layer lines must be treated honestly as part of the manufacturing language or reduced through process control

## Visual Hierarchy

1. Customer job
2. Attachment and use affordance
3. Module family
4. Personal color or edition
5. Brand mark

The logo should not be the loudest evidence that products belong together.

## Accessibility and Inclusion

- Do not rely on color alone for Zone, orientation, or compatibility.
- Provide tactile or shape cues where they improve comprehension.
- Maintain readable package and digital contrast.
- Avoid excessive force and unexplained two-hand sequences.
- Test with left- and right-handed use where relevant.
- Use plain language before proprietary terminology.

## Design Review Questions

- Is the job legible before the brand is explained?
- Does it feel related to the platform without copying an existing silhouette?
- Is attachment direction clear?
- Is the confirmation event meaningful or merely decorative?
- Can neutral and vivid modules coexist?
- Does the design remain coherent outside a styled photograph?
- Does form support the stated environment and cleaning behavior?
- Would removing the logo still leave a recognizable family experience?
