# FUN-TASTIC Brand Principles

Checked: 2026-07-18

## The Ten-Year Rules

### 1. Prior ownership becomes more valuable

Every new module should improve the capability, arrangement, expression, or serviceability of something the customer already owns.

### 2. Familiarity compounds

The attachment logic, compatibility mark, naming hierarchy, and onboarding pattern should remain recognizable even when the Zone or function changes.

### 3. Immediate value comes first

A module cannot be justified only by a future ecosystem. It must solve a current customer job by itself or as an explicit Starter Kit component.

### 4. The interface is a promise

The company may improve the standard, but it must never silently strand earlier Bases or modules.

### 5. One brand does not mean one material

Dry, wet, hot, portable, mounted, and higher-load contexts require honest Base and material classes while preserving the common brand grammar.

### 6. Joy comes from mastery

The interaction should feel satisfying because it becomes clear and fluent, not because it adds unnecessary steps.

### 7. Collection must remain useful

Completion, color, and collaboration may enhance purchase desire, but each item must still provide utility, expression, service, or meaningful display value.

### 8. Curating is a product function

The brand must help the customer find the right module. A large unexplained catalog weakens the platform.

### 9. Quiet and expressive can coexist

Neutral Bases create continuity; modules provide controlled identity. The system should feel friendly without becoming childish or visually exhausting.

### 10. Trust outlives novelty

Compatibility support, honest claims, replacement parts, and predictable naming outrank a seasonal release.

## Operating Tests

Every major decision should answer:

- Does this increase cumulative value?
- Does it preserve familiar behavior?
- Is the customer job clear without platform jargon?
- Is the environment claim honest?
- Does this create useful expansion rather than catalog noise?
- Can customers find, understand, maintain, and replace it?
- Would the decision still make sense without a limited-edition label?

## Prohibited Shortcuts

- Visual imitation of LEGO studs, proportions, minifigure cues, or trade dress
- Silent interface revisions
- Essential functionality available only through random purchase
- Artificial shortage presented as customer value
- Color-only duplication counted as innovation
- A product called a module without real platform participation
- Wet, heat, load, or compatibility claims transferred without validation
- Packaging that hides necessary compatibility or service information
